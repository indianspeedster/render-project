A description of the Rains and Autumn

श्रीमद्भागवतमहापुराणम्

श्री शुक उवाच

तयोस्तदद्भुतं कर्म दावाग्नेर्मोक्षमात्मनः ।

गोपाः स्त्रीभ्यः समाचख्युः प्रलम्बवधमेव च ॥10.20.1॥

Word-by-word meaning: श्री शुकः उवाच Shri Shuka said, तयोः of those two (Krishna and Balarama), तत्-अद्भुतम् that wonderful, कर्म activity/deed, दावाग्नेः from the forest fire, मोक्षम् the deliverance, आत्मनः of themselves, गोपाः the cowherd boys, स्त्रीभ्यः to the women (the gopis and mothers), समाचख्युः they fully described, प्रलम्ब-वधम् the killing of Pralamba, एव च and also

Anvaya: श्रीशुकः उवाच — गोपाः आत्मनः दावाग्नेः मोक्षं प्रलम्बवधम् एव च तयोः तत् अद्भुतं कर्म स्त्रीभ्यः समाचख्युः।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: श्री शुकः उवाच Shri Shuka said, गोपाः the cowherd boys, आत्मनः दावाग्नेः मोक्षं their own deliverance from the forest fire, प्रलम्बवधम् एव च and also the killing of Pralamba, तयोः तत् अद्भुतं कर्म (and) that wonderful deed of those two, स्त्रीभ्यः समाचख्युः described to the women.

Simple meaning: Shri Shuka said: Upon returning to the village, the cowherd boys gave a full account to the women of Vraja about how Balarama had killed the asura Pralamba and how Krishna had miraculously delivered them all from the fierce forest fire.

गोपवृद्धाश्च गोप्यश्च तदुपाकर्ण्य विस्मिताः ।

मेनिरे देवप्रवरौ कृष्णरामौ व्रजं गतौ ॥10.20.2॥

Word-by-word meaning: गोप-वृद्धाः the elderly cowherd men, च and, गोप्यः the cowherd women (gopis), च and, तत्-उपाकर्ण्य hearing that, विस्मिताः astonished, मेनिरे they considered, देव-प्रवरौ the two best of demigods, कृष्ण-रामौ Krishna and Balarama, व्रजम् to Vraja, गतौ having come

Anvaya: गोपवृद्धाः गोप्यः च तत् उपाकर्ण्य विस्मिताः (सन्तः), व्रजं गतौ कृष्णरामौ देवप्रवरौ मेनिरे।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: गोपवृद्धाः गोप्यः च The elderly cowherd men and the cowherd women, तत् उपाकर्ण्य hearing that, विस्मिताः being astonished, व्रजं गतौ कृष्णरामौ Krishna and Balarama, who had come to Vraja, देवप्रवरौ मेनिरे considered them to be the greatest of demigods.

Simple meaning: Hearing the amazing accounts from the boys, both the elderly men and the women of Vraja were struck with wonder. They began to believe that Krishna and Balarama were actually the most exalted of demigods who had descended to their village.

ततः प्रावर्तत प्रावृट् सर्वसत्त्वसमुद्भवा ।

विद्योतमानपरिधिर्विस्फूर्जितनभस्तला ॥10.20.3॥

Word-by-word meaning: ततः then, प्रावर्तत began/set in, प्रावृट् the rainy season, सर्व-सत्त्व-समुद्भवा which causes the birth/flourishing of all living beings, विद्योतमान-परिधिः whose horizon was illuminated (by lightning), विस्फूर्जित-नभस्तला whose sky resounded (with thunder).

Anvaya: ततः सर्वसत्त्वसमुद्भवा विद्योतमानपरिधिः विस्फूर्जितनभस्तला प्रावृट् प्रावर्तत।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: ततः Then, सर्वसत्त्वसमुद्भवा (the season) which brings about the flourishing of all living beings, विद्योतमानपरिधिः whose quarters are illuminated by lightning, विस्फूर्जितनभस्तला whose sky is filled with the sound of thunder, प्रावृट् प्रावर्तत the rainy season began.

Simple meaning: Then the rainy season began—the time of year that brings life and growth to all living creatures. The horizon flashed with constant lightning, and the entire sky resounded with the deep vibration of thunder.

सान्द्रलीलाम्बुदैर्व्योम सविद्युत्स्तनयित्नुभिः ।

अस्पष्टज्योतिराच्छन्नं ब्रह्मेव सगुणं बभौ ॥10.20.4॥

Word-by-word meaning: सान्द्र-नील-अम्बुदैः by dense, dark-blue clouds, व्योम the sky, स-विद्युत्-स्तनयित्नुभिः accompanied by lightning and thunder, अस्पष्ट-ज्योतिः with its lights (the sun, moon, and stars) indistinct, आच्छन्नम् covered, ब्रह्म इव like the Brahman (spirit), स-गुणम् possessing qualities (manifest), बभौ it appeared

Anvaya: सविद्युत्स्तनयित्नुभिः सान्द्रनीलाम्बुदैः आच्छन्नम् अस्पष्टज्योतिः व्योम सगुणं ब्रह्म इव बभौ।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: सविद्युत्स्तनयित्नुभिः सान्द्रनीलाम्बुदैः By dense dark clouds accompanied by lightning and thunder, आच्छन्नम् covered, अस्पष्टज्योतिः its lights being obscured, व्योम the sky, सगुणं ब्रह्म इव बभौ appeared like the Brahman manifested with qualities.

Simple meaning: The sky was completely covered by thick, dark-blue clouds that rumbled with thunder and flashed with lightning. With the sun and stars obscured from view, the overcast sky appeared like the Brahman (spirit) with the three material qualities (Jivatma with its light of wisdom obscured by the Sattva, Rajas and Tamas corresponding in the case of the sky to the flashes of lightning, thunder and the clouds).

अष्टौ मासान् निपीतं यद् भूम्याश्चोदमयं वसु ।

स्वगोभिर्मोक्तुमारेभे पर्जन्यः काल आगते ॥10.20.5॥

Word-by-word meaning: अष्टौ eight, मासान् months, निपीतम् drunk up, यत् which, भूम्याः from the earth, उद-मयम् consisting of water, वसु wealth/substance, स्व-गोभिः by it’s waters, मोक्तुम् to release, आरेभे began, पर्जन्यः the rain-god/the cloud, काले in due time, आगते having arrived.

गो - बाणः, इन्द्रस्य वज्रायुधम्, जलम्, किरणः, नेत्रम्, पशुः, स्वर्गः, वचनम्, दिक्, भूमिः - जलम् in this context src. Shabdapath, Ashtadhyayi

Anvaya: यत् भूम्याः उदमयं वसु अष्टौ मासान् निपीतं, (तत्) काल आगते पर्जन्यः स्वगोभिः मोक्तुम् आरेभे।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: यत् भूम्याः उदमयं वसु That watery wealth of the earth, अष्टौ मासान् निपीतं which was drunk for eight months, (तत्) काल आगते that, when the time arrived, पर्जन्यः the rain-cloud, स्वगोभिः by his own waters, मोक्तुम् आरेभे began to release.

Simple meaning: For eight months, the clouds had drunk up the earth's watery wealth. Now that the proper season had arrived, the clouds began to release that accumulated moisture back to the earth.

तडित्वन्तो महामेघाश्चण्डश्वसनवेपिताः ।

प्रीणनं जीवनं ह्यस्य मुमुचुः करुणा इव ॥10.20.6॥

Word-by-word meaning: तडित्वन्तः possessing lightning, महा-मेघाः the great clouds, चण्ड-श्वसन-वेपिताः shaken by fierce winds, प्रीणनम् pleasing/refreshing, जीवनम् the life-giving (water), हि indeed, अस्य of this (the world/earth), मुमुचुः they released, करुणाः compassionate (persons), इव like

Anvaya: चण्डश्वसनवेपिताः तडित्वन्तः महामेघाः करुणाः इव अस्य (जगतः) प्रीणनं जीवनं हि मुमुचुः।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: चण्डश्वसनवेपिताः Shaken by fierce winds, तडित्वन्तः possessing lightning, महामेघाः the great clouds, करुणाः इव like compassionate persons, अस्य (जगतः) of this world, प्रीणनं जीवनं refreshing and life-giving (water), हि मुमुचुः indeed released.

Simple meaning: Tossed and shaken by fierce winds, the great lightning-charged clouds released their refreshing, life-giving water upon the earth just as compassionate souls who, being moved by the "winds" of others' distress, give away their very life-substance to bring relief to the world.

तपःकृशा देवमीढा आसीद् वर्षीयसी मही ।

यथैव काम्यतपसस्तनुः सम्प्राप्य तत्फलम् ॥10.20.7॥

Word-by-word meaning: तपः-कृशा emaciated by austerities (heat), देव-मीढा moistened by the rain-god, आसीत् became, वर्षीयसी very lush/full/flourishing, मही the earth, यथा just as, एव certainly, काम्य-तपसः of one performing austerities for a desired result, तनुः the body, सम्प्राप्य having attained, तत्-फलम् the fruit of that (austerity)

Anvaya: तपःकृशा मही देवमीढा (सती) – यथा एव काम्यतपसः तनुः तत्फलं सम्प्राप्य – वर्षीयासी आसीत्।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: तपःकृशा मही The earth, emaciated by the heat of summer, देवमीढा (सती) having been moistened by the rain-god, यथा एव just as, काम्यतपसः तनुः the body of one who has performed austerities, तत्फलं सम्प्राप्य having attained the desired fruit, वर्षीयसी आसीत् became full and lush.

Simple meaning: The earth, which had been dried and emaciated by the intense summer heat, became lush and vibrant once it was moistened by the rain, just as the body of a person who, after undergoing austerities to achieve a goal, finally becomes healthy and full of life upon receiving the desired result.

Key Comparisons

  • The Earth's Summer State: Like the thin, worn body of someone performing hard penance (tapas).

  • The Rain: Like the attainment of the specific goal or reward (phalam).

  • The Earth's Monsoon State: Like the rejuvenation of a person who is finally satisfied and nourished after their hard work.

निशामुखेषु खद्योतास्तमसा भान्ति न ग्रहाः ।

यथा पापेन पाखण्डा न हि वेदाः कलौ युगे ॥10.20.8॥

Word-by-word meaning: निशा-मुखेषु at the beginning of the nights, खद्योताः fireflies, तमसा by the darkness, भान्ति shine, न not, ग्रहाः the planets/stars, यथा just as, पापेन by sin/wickedness, पाखण्डाः heretics/false doctrines, न not, हि indeed, वेदाः the Vedas, कलौ युगे in the age of Kali

Anvaya: निशामुखेषु तमसा खद्योताः भान्ति, न (तु) ग्रहाः, यथा कलौ युगे पापेन पाखण्डाः (भान्ति), न हि वेदाः।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: निशामुखेषु In the twilight of the nights, तमसा by the darkness, खद्योताः भान्ति fireflies shine, न (तु) ग्रहाः but not the stars, यथा just as, कलौ युगे in the Kali-yuga, पापेन by sin, पाखण्डाः (भान्ति) heretics shine, न हि वेदाः and indeed not the Vedas.

Simple meaning: In the darkness of the rainy season nights, fireflies are visible while the stars and planets are hidden, just as in the Age of Kali, the darkness of sin allows false, heretical doctrines to flourish and gain prominence, while the true light of the Vedas remains obscured.

Comparison breakdown

  • The Darkness: Represents the ignorance and sin of the Kali-yuga.

  • The Fireflies: Represent false teachers or heretics (pakhandas) who have a small, flickering light but no real substance.

  • The Stars/Planets: Represent the Vedas and eternal truths, which are vast and brilliant but become hidden by the prevailing "clouds" of the age.

श्रुत्वा पर्जन्यनिनदं मण्डूका व्यसृजन् गिरः।

तूष्णीं शयानाः प्राग् यद्वद् ब्राह्मणा नियमात्यये ॥10.20.9॥

Word-by-word meaning: श्रुत्वा hearing, पर्जन्य-निनदम् the sound of the rain-cloud (thunder), मण्डूकाः the frogs, व्यसृजन् emitted/uttered, गिरः sounds/voices, तूष्णीम् silently, शयानाः lying down/resting, प्राक् before, यत्-वत् just as, ब्राह्मणाः brahmanas, नियम-अत्यये at the end of their vow (or at the start of their routine).

Anvaya: प्राक् तूष्णीं शयानाः मण्डूकाः पर्जन्यनिनदं श्रुत्वा गिरः व्यसृजन्, यद्वत् नियमात्यये ब्राह्मणाः (गिरः व्यसृजन्)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: प्राक् तूष्णीं शयानाः मण्डूकाः The frogs who were previously lying silent, पर्जन्यनिनदं श्रुत्वा hearing the thunder of the rain-cloud, गिरः व्यसृजन् uttered their voices, यद्वत् just as, नियमात्यये at the end of a silent vow (or at the break of dawn), ब्राह्मणाः (गिरः व्यसृजन्) brahmanas recite their chants.

Simple meaning: Having remained silent throughout the summer, the frogs began to croak loudly upon hearing the rumbling of the clouds just as Vedic students or brahmanas who, after finishing a silent vow or at the proper hour of study, begin their loud and rhythmic recitation of the scriptures.

Analysis of the Metaphor: The poet compares the natural sound of the frogs in the rainy season to the ritualistic chanting of the Vedas. In many traditional commentaries, नियमात्यये is interpreted as the end of the night (dawn), when brahmanas break their silence to begin the morning prayers. The "trigger" for the frogs is the thunder, just as the "trigger" for the students is the command of the teacher or the specific time of the day.

आसन्नुत्पथवाहिन्यः क्षुद्रनद्योऽनुशुष्यतीः ।

पुंसो यथास्वतन्त्रस्य देहद्रविणसम्पदः ॥10.20.10॥

Word-by-word meaning: आसन् became, उत्पथ-वाहिन्यः flowing out of their paths (overflowing the banks), क्षुद्र-नद्यः the small rivers/streams, अनुशुष्यतीः which had been dried up, पुंसः of a person, यथा just as, अ-स्वतन्त्रस्य who is not self-controlled (slave to senses), देह-द्रविण-सम्पदः the riches of the body and wealth

Anvaya: अनुशुष्यतीः क्षुद्रनद्यः उत्पथवाहिन्यः आसन्, यथा अस्वतन्त्रस्य पुंसः देहद्रविणसम्पदः (भवन्ति)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: अनुशुष्यतीः क्षुद्रनद्यः The small streams that had dried up, उत्पथवाहिन्यः आसन् began to overflow their paths, यथा just as, अस्वतन्त्रस्य पुंसः of a person who is not self-controlled, देहद्रविणसम्पदः (भवन्ति) the body and his wealth (are used improperly).

Simple meaning: The tiny streams that had completely dried up during the summer suddenly overflowed their banks with the rains just as a man who lacks self-control, as soon as he acquires a little physical strength or a bit of wealth, he ignores proper boundaries and "overflows" into reckless or sinful behavior.

Philosophical Parallel

  • The Small Streams: Represent a person with a shallow or immature character.

  • The Rain/Water: Represents sudden prosperity, health, or sensory objects.

  • Overflowing the Banks (उत्पथवाहिन्यः): Represents deviating from the path of dharma (उत्पथ literally means "off the road"). Just as a small stream cannot contain a sudden influx of water, an undisciplined person cannot handle sudden success without losing their moral center.

हरिता हरिभिः शष्पैरिन्द्रगोपैश्च लोहिताः ।

उच्छिलीन्ध्रकृतच्छाया नृणां श्रीरिव भूरभूत् ॥10.20.11॥

Word-by-word meaning: हरिता green, हरिभिः with green, शष्पैः by the young grass, इन्द्र-गोपैः with Indragopa insects (velvet mites/ladybugs), च and, लोहिताः red, उच्छिलीन्ध्र-कृत-छाया with the shade/beauty created by burgeoning mushrooms, नृणाम् of men, श्रीः prosperity/wealth, इव like, भूः the earth, अभूत became

Anvaya: हरिभिः शष्पैः हरिता, इन्द्रगोपैः लोहिता, उच्छिलीन्ध्रकृतच्छाया च भूः, नृणां श्रीः इव अभूत्।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: हरिभिः शष्पैः हरिता Green with the young blades of grass, इन्द्रगोपैः लोहिता red with Indragopa insects, उच्छिलीन्ध्रकृतच्छाया च and possessing the beauty created by emerging mushrooms, भूः the earth, नृणां श्रीः इव अभूत् became like the prosperity of men.

Simple meaning: The earth became vibrant green with new grass and was dotted with red Indragopa insects. With mushrooms springing up everywhere to add to its beauty, the earth looked like a wealthy person whose prosperity is displayed through various riches and adornments.

Comparison breakdown

  • Green Grass: Like the foundation of wealth or green garments.

  • Indragopa (Red Insects): Like precious red jewels (rubies) or ornaments.

  • Mushrooms: Like the umbrellas or canopies (chhatra) that signify royal or high status.

  • The Earth: Compared to a person who has suddenly come into great fortune (shri).

क्षेत्राणि सस्यसम्पद्भिः कर्षकाणां मुदं ददुः ।

धनिनामुपतापं च दैवाधीनमजानताम् ॥10.20.12॥

Word-by-word meaning: क्षेत्राणि the fields, सस्य-सम्पद्भिः with their wealth of crops, कर्षकाणाम् of the farmers, मुदम् joy, ददुः gave, धनिनाम् of the wealthy (landlords/moneylenders), उपतापम् distress/remorse, च and, दैव-अधीनम् (that prosperity is) dependent on destiny, अजानताम् who do not understand

Anvaya: सस्यसम्पद्भिः क्षेत्राणि कर्षकाणां मुदं ददुः, (परन्तु) दैवाधीनम् अजानतां धनिनाम् उपतापं च (ददुः)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: सस्यसम्पद्भिः क्षेत्राणि The fields, with their abundance of crops, कर्षकाणां मुदं ददुः gave joy to the farmers, (परन्तु) दैवाधीनम् अजानतां but to those who do not understand that it is all dependent on destiny, धनिनाम् उपतापं च (ददुः) they gave distress to the wealthy people.

Simple meaning: The fields, now lush with an abundance of crops, brought great joy to the hardworking farmers. However, that same prosperity caused distress to the wealthy—those who, failing to realize that all success ultimately depends on the will of Providence, felt envious or regretted not being able to extract more from the abundance.

Philosophical Insight

The verse highlights two different reactions to prosperity:

  • The Farmers: Represent the humble who rejoice in the fruit of their labor as a blessing.

  • The Wealthy (धनिनाम्): Represent those who view resources through the lens of ego and control. Because they do not acknowledge the divine hand (दैवाधीनम्) in the growth of crops, they suffer from the "heat" (उपतापं) of greed or envy when they see others prosper.

Simple meaning: All living beings—whether they lived in the water or on the land—took on a beautiful and refreshed appearance by making use of the fresh rainwater. This is just like a person who becomes spiritually radiant and beautiful in character by engaging in the service of the Supreme Lord, Hari.

The Parallel of "निषेवया"

The poet uses the word निषेवया (service/use/resorting to) to draw a profound spiritual analogy:

  • The Fresh Rain (नव-वारि): Just as the parched creatures of the earth are revitalized and beautified by the falling rain, the parched soul is revitalized by divine service.

  • The Result (रुचिरं रूपं): Just as the physical form of animals and plants becomes bright and healthy with water, the "form" of a devotee becomes attractive and graceful through the cultivation of devotion.

Philosophical Comparison

  • The Ocean: Represents the mind of the practitioner.

  • The Rivers: Represent the various sense objects (vishaya) that flow into the consciousness.

  • The Wind: Represents the force of previous habits or passions.

  • The Agitation (चुक्षुभे): Just as the ocean cannot remain calm when too many turbulent rivers enter it during the rains, a yogi who has not yet reached maturity loses his equilibrium when he encounters worldly attractions.

Simple meaning: Just as mountains remain unmoved even when struck by the most powerful torrents of rain, devotees whose minds are fully absorbed in the Supreme Lord remain undisturbed even when overwhelmed by great calamities or worldly troubles.

Philosophical Depth

  • The Mountains: Represent the sturdiness of a realized soul (sthitaprajña).

  • The Rain Torrents: Represent the "slings and arrows" of outrageous fortune—external pressures that would break a lesser structure.

  • Adhokshaja-chetasah: This refers to those whose consciousness is fixed on Adhokshaja (the Lord who is beyond the reach of material senses). Because their foundation is transcendental, worldly "weather" (pain, loss, or hardship) cannot cause them internal distress.

The Parallel of Neglect

This verse draws a sharp comparison between physical infrastructure and intellectual/spiritual tradition:

  • The Roads: Represent the paths of knowledge or dharma.

  • The Overgrowing Grass: Represents the distractions, laziness, or the "weeds" of worldly life that obscure the path.

  • The Brahmanas/Dvijas: Act as the custodians. Just as a road needs constant foot traffic and maintenance to stay clear, the Vedas (Shrutis) require constant recitation and contemplation to remain "visible" and accessible to society.

  • Kala-hatah: This suggests that without the "maintenance" of active study, time itself acts as a destructive force that erases truth.

लोकबन्धुषु मेघेषु विद्युतश्चलसौहृदाः ।

स्थैर्यं न चक्रुः कामिन्यः पुरुषेषु गुणिष्विव ॥10.20.17॥

Word-by-word meaning: लोक-बन्धुषु in the friends of the world, मेघेषु in the clouds, विद्युतः the lightnings, चल-सौहृदाः having fickle friendship, स्थैर्यम् stability/steadfastness, न not, चक्रुः did (make), कामिन्यः lustful/fickle women, पुरुषेषु in men, गुणिषु possessing virtuous qualities, इव like

Anvaya: लोकबन्धुषु मेघेषु चलसौहृदाः विद्युतः स्थैर्यं न चक्रुः, गुणिषु पुरुषेषु कामिन्यः इव।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: लोकबन्धुषु मेघेषु In the clouds, which are the friends of the world, चलसौहृदाः विद्युतः the lightnings of flickering friendship, स्थैर्यं न चक्रुः did not remain steady, गुणिषु पुरुषेषु (यथा) in virtuous men, कामिन्यः इव like fickle women (do not remain steady).

Simple meaning: The clouds are the true friends of the world because they bring life-giving rain, yet the lightning within them flickers and disappears instantly. This instability of lightning is compared to fickle-minded women who cannot remain steady or loyal in their affection, even when they are with virtuous and noble men.

Philosophical Comparison

  • The Clouds (Loka-bandhu): Represent steady, virtuous, and giving personalities (gunis). They perform their duty of nourishing the world without bias.

  • The Lightning (Vidyut): Represents momentary attraction or fickle affection.

  • The Metaphor: The poet observes that even though the cloud is a "friend to all" and a solid provider, the lightning's nature is to be transient. Similarly, the verse suggests that a person with a restless or lustful heart (कामिन्यः) cannot appreciate or stay committed to a person of high character, as their own nature is inherently unstable.

धनुर्वियति माहेन्द्रं निर्गुणं च गुणिन्यभात् ।

व्यक्ते गुणव्यतिकरेऽगुणवान् पुरुषो यथा ॥10.20.18॥

Word-by-word meaning: धनुः a bow, वियति in the sky, माहेन्द्रम् of Indra, निर्गुणम् without a string (also: without material qualities), च and, गुणिनी (in the sky) which possessed clouds (also: possessed of material qualities), अभात् appeared, व्यक्ते in the manifested, गुण-व्यतिकरे in the transformation/interaction of the gunas, अ-गुणवान् free from material qualities, पुरुषः the Supreme Person, यथा just as

Anvaya: गुणिनि वियति निर्गुणं माहेन्द्रं धनुः अभात्, यथा व्यक्ते गुणव्यतिकरे अगुणवान् पुरुषः (आभाति)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: गुणिनि वियति In the sky possessed of clouds (qualities), निर्गुणं माहेन्द्रं धनुः the stringless bow of Indra (the rainbow), अभात् appeared, यथा just as, व्यक्ते गुणव्यतिकरे in the manifest interaction of material qualities, अगुणवान् पुरुषः the non-material Supreme Person (appears).

Simple meaning: A rainbow appeared in the sky, which was heavy with clouds. Although the sky was "filled with qualities" (clouds/colors), the rainbow itself was nirguna (without a physical bowstring). This is like the Supreme Soul (Purusha), who appears within this world of manifested material qualities (gunas) yet remains entirely free from them and untouched by them.

The Philosophical Double Entendre (Shlesha)

This verse is a masterpiece of Sanskrit wordplay, using the word Guna in two different senses:

  1. Technical sense: A "string" for a bow.

  2. Philosophical sense: The three "modes" of material nature (Sattva, Rajas, Tamas).

Element Material Level Spiritual Level
The Sky Gunini: Filled with clouds and colors. Prakriti: The manifest world of the three modes.
The Rainbow Nirgunam: A bow without a string. The Purusha: The Lord who is Nirguna (beyond modes).
The Interaction Guna-vyatikare: The mixture of light and water. Guna-vyatikare: The interaction of material nature.

Just as the rainbow is seen in the sky but is not "strung" or tied to the physical laws of a wooden bow, the Divine appears within the material creation but is never bound by its limitations.

न रराजोडुपश्छन्नः स्वज्योत्स्नाराजितैर्घनैः ।

अहंमत्या भासितया स्वभासा पुरुषो यथा ॥10.20.19॥

Word-by-word meaning: न not, रराज shone, उडुपः the moon, छन्नः covered, स्व-ज्योत्स्ना-राजितैः which were illuminated by its own moonlight, घनैः by the clouds, अहम्-मत्या by the false ego (the "I-sense"), भासितया which is manifested/illuminated, स्व-भासा by His own light, पुरुषः the Supreme Person, यथा just as

Anvaya: स्वज्योत्स्नाराजितैः घनैः छन्नः उडुपः न रराज, यथा स्वभासा भासितया अहंमत्या (छन्नः) पुरुषः (न राजते)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: स्वज्योत्स्नाराजितैः घनैः छन्नः Covered by clouds that are illuminated by its own moonlight, उडुपः न रराज the moon did not shine (clearly), यथा just as, स्वभासा भासितया अहंमत्या by the false ego which is illuminated by His own light, (छन्नः) पुरुषः the Supreme Soul (is not seen to shine).

Simple meaning: The moon was hidden behind clouds, even though those very clouds were only visible because they were lit up by the moon's own light. In the same way, the Supreme Soul remains hidden behind the false ego (ahankara), even though the ego itself only possesses the power of consciousness because it is illuminated by the light of that very Soul.

Philosophical Comparison

This verse describes the "Irony of the Ego" through a visual metaphor:

  • The Moon: Represents the Atman or Purusha (the source of all light/consciousness).

  • The Clouds: Represent Ahankara (the false ego).

  • The Paradox: We see the "clouds" of our ego and think they are the reality, forgetting that they only have "light" (awareness) because the Soul is behind them. Just as the clouds obscure the moon that gives them their glow, the ego obscures the Divine that gives it life.

मेघागमोत्सवा हृष्टाः प्रत्यनन्दञ्छिखण्डिनः ।

Word-by-word meaning: मेघ-आगम-उत्सवाः for whom the arrival of clouds is a festival, हृष्टाः joyful, प्रत्यनन्दन् welcomed with joy, शिखण्डिनः the peacocks, गृहेषु in houses, तप्ताः distressed, निर्विण्णाः dejected/world-weary, यथा just as, अच्युत-जन-आगमे upon the arrival of the devotees of Lord Achyuta (Krishna)

Anvaya: मेघागमोत्सवाः हृष्टाः शिखण्डिनः प्रत्यनन्दन्, यथा गृहेषु तप्ताः निर्विण्णाः अच्युतजनागमे (प्रत्यनन्दन्ति)।

Philosophical Comparison

  • The Peacocks: Represent the parched souls of this world.

  • The Clouds: Represent the devotees or the "mercy" they carry.

  • The Distress (taptah/nirvinnah): The summer heat represents the "threefold miseries" (tapa-traya) of worldly existence. The three-fold miseries (tapa-traya) of material existence are defined as: Adhyatmika (bodily and mental suffering), Adhibhautika (suffering caused by other living entities), and Adhidaivika (suffering caused by higher forces/nature).

  • The Joy: Just as rain is the only thing that can cool the earth and make the peacock dance, the association of saintly people (sadhu-sanga) is described as the only true relief for a heart weary of material pursuits.

  • Nanatma-murtayah: Before the rain, all trees looked similarly dry and skeletal. After the rain, their individual natures (various leaves and flowers) became manifest. Similarly, when people's basic needs or intense desires are met, their individual personalities and physical vitality flourish in distinct ways.

Philosophical Comparison

  • The Restless Lakes: Represent a home environment filled with stress, noise, and worldly duties (अशान्त-कृत्येषु).

  • The Cranes (सारसाः): Usually symbols of grace, here they represent those who "settle" for less than ideal conditions due to habit or necessity.

  • Durashayah: This refers to "evil hopes" or deep-seated material attachments. Just as the bird doesn't fly away to a more peaceful spot, the worldly person (ग्राम्याः) stays entangled in a stressful life because they are driven by the hope of future sensory pleasure or a sense of "mine-ness" regarding their home.

जलौघैर्निरभिद्यन्त सेतवो वर्षतीश्वरे ।

पाखण्डिनामसद्वादैर्वेदमार्गाः कलौ यथा ॥10.20.23॥

Word-by-word meaning: जल-ओघैः by the floods of water, निरभिद्यन्त were broken/washed away, सेतवः the bridges/dams, वर्षति (while) raining, ईश्वरे (while) the Lord (the sky/Indra) (was raining), पाखण्डिनाम् of the heretics, असत्-वादैः by the false arguments/atheistic theories, वेद-मार्गाः the paths of the Vedas, कलौ in the age of Kali, यथा just as

Anvaya: ईश्वरे वर्षति (सति) जलौघैः सेतवः निरभिद्यन्त, यथा कलौ पाखण्डिनाम् असद्वादैः वेदमार्गाः (निरभिद्यन्ते)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: ईश्वरे वर्षति (सति) While the Lord (the rain-god) was pouring rain, जलौघैः by the floods of water, सेतवः निरभिद्यन्त the dams were broken, यथा just as, कलौ in the Kali-yuga, पाखण्डिनाम् असद्वादैः by the false arguments of heretics, वेदमार्गाः (निरभिद्यन्ते) the paths of the Vedas are broken.

Simple meaning: Under the heavy downpour of the rainy season, the dams and bridges that regulate the water were washed away by the sheer force of the floods. This is exactly like the Age of Kali, where the regulated paths of Vedic life and traditional morality are broken down by the powerful, yet false and atheistic, arguments of heretical teachers.

Philosophical Comparison

  • The Dams (Setavah): In Sanskrit literature, a setu is not just a bridge, but a boundary or a dam that keeps things in their proper place. It represents the boundaries of Dharma.

  • The Floods: Represent the overwhelming force of material passion and uncontrolled desires during the rainy season.

  • The Heretics (Pakhandas): Those who preach against the Vedas. Their "false arguments" (असत्-वादैः) act like a flood that erases the "roads" of disciplined spiritual life, making it difficult for the common person to follow the correct path.

व्यमुञ्चन् वायुभिर्नुन्ना भूतेभ्योऽथामृतं घनाः ।

यथाऽऽशिषो विश्पतयः काले काले द्विजेरिताः ॥10.20.24॥

Word-by-word meaning: व्यमुञ्चन् released/showered, वायुभिः by the winds, नुन्नाः driven/impelled, भूतेभ्यः for the living beings, अथ then, अमृतम् nectar (water), घनाः the clouds, यथा just as, आशिषः blessings, विश्-पतयः the rulers of the people (kings), काले काले at the appropriate times, द्विज-ईरिताः prompted by the brahmanas

Anvaya: अथ वायुभिः नुन्नाः घनाः भूतेभ्यः अमृतं व्यमुञ्चन्, यथा द्विजईरिताः विश्पतयः काले काले आशिषः (व्यमुञ्चन्ति)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: अथ Then, वायुभिः नुन्नाः driven by the winds, घनाः the clouds, भूतेभ्यः अमृतं व्यमुञ्चन् showered nectar for all living beings, यथा just as, द्विजईरिताः prompted by the brahmanas, विश्पतयः the kings, काले काले at the appropriate times, आशिषः (व्यमुञ्चन्ति) release blessings (charity/protection).

Simple meaning: Driven by the winds, the clouds showered their life-giving, nectar-like water upon all living creatures. This act of the clouds is like that of noble kings who, encouraged and guided by learned brahmanas, bestow charity and protective blessings upon their subjects at the most needed times.

The Comparison of Governance

The poet creates a parallel between the natural ecosystem and an ideal social structure:

  • The Clouds (घनाः): Represent the Kings or leaders (विश्पतयः) who hold the resources of the state.

  • The Winds (वायुभिः): Represent the Brahmanas or wise counselors (द्विजईरिताः) who do not provide the "water" themselves but provide the necessary impulse and guidance for the "clouds" to act.

  • The Nectar (अमृतं): Represents the tax-wealth returned to the people as welfare, or the general safety and prosperity provided by the state.

  • The Beneficiaries (भूतेभ्यः): Just as the rain doesn't discriminate between a blade of grass and a great tree, a righteous king provides for all levels of society.

एवं वनं तद् वर्षिष्ठं पक्वखर्जूरजम्बूमत् ।

गोगोपालैर्वृतो रन्तुं सबलः प्राविश्धरिः ॥10.20.25॥

Word-by-word meaning: एवम् in this way, वनम् the forest, तत् that (Vrindavana), वर्षिष्ठम् lush/at its peak due to the rainy season, पक्व-खर्जूर-जम्बू-मत् containing ripe dates and jambu fruits, गो-गोपालैः by the cows and cowherd boys, वृतः surrounded, रन्तुम् to play/enjoy, स-बलः along with Balarama, प्राविशत् entered, हरिः Lord Krishna

Anvaya: एवं (दृश्यं) पक्वखर्जूरजम्बूमत् वर्षिष्ठं तत् वनं सबलः हरिः गोगोपालैः वृतः रन्तुं प्राविशत्।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: एवं In this way, पक्वखर्जूरजम्बूमत् possessing ripe dates and jambu fruits, वर्षिष्ठं तत् वनं that forest, which was in its most lush state, सबलः हरिः Lord Hari, together with Balarama, गोगोपालैः वृतः surrounded by the cows and cowherds, रन्तुं प्राविशत् entered to play.

Simple meaning: In this manner, the forest of Vrindavana had become opulently lush and beautiful, with its trees laden with ripe dates and jambu fruits. Lord Krishna, accompanied by Balarama and surrounded by His cows and cowherd friends, entered that vibrant forest to enjoy His pastimes.

Contextual Significance

  • वर्षिष्ठम्: This term implies the forest was at its "most excellent" or "most abundant" state due to the heavy rains (varsha). It sets the stage for the intimate pastimes of Krishna in nature.

  • The Fruits (खर्जूर/जम्बू): These specific fruits signal the peak of the season. They provide the practical "props" for the play (rantum) of the cowherd boys, who would pick and share them.

  • Transition: This verse concludes the long series of poetic metaphors comparing the rainy season to spiritual and moral concepts, returning the focus to the physical presence of Hari (Krishna) within that transformed landscape.

धेनवो मन्दगामिन्य ऊधोभारेण भूयसा ।

ययुर्भगवताऽऽहूता द्रुतं प्रीत्या स्नुतस्तनीः ॥10.20.26॥

Word-by-word meaning: धेनवः the cows, मन्द-गामिन्यः moving slowly, ऊधः-भारेण due to the weight of their udders, भूयसा very great, ययुः went, भगवता by the Supreme Lord (Krishna), आहूताः called, द्रुतम् quickly, प्रीत्या out of love, स्नुत-स्तनीः with milk dripping from their nipples.

Anvaya: भूयसा ऊधोभारेण मन्दगामिन्यः (अपि) धेनवः भगवता आहूताः (सत्यः) प्रीत्या स्नुतस्तनीः (सत्यः) द्रुतं ययुः।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: भूयसा ऊधोभारेण Due to the great weight of their udders, मन्दगामिन्यः (अपि) even though they were moving slowly, धेनवः the cows, भगवता आहूताः (सत्यः) being called by the Lord, प्रीत्या out of affection, स्नुतस्तनीः with milk dripping from their udders, द्रुतं ययुः went quickly.

Simple meaning: Because their udders were heavy and full of milk, the cows were naturally moving slowly. However, as soon as Lord Krishna called out to them, they were so overcome with love that milk began to flow spontaneously from them, and they ran toward Him with great speed, forgetting the weight they carried.

वनौकसः प्रमुदिता वनराजीर्मधुच्युतः ।

जलधारा गिरेर्नादानासन्ना ददृशे गुहाः ॥10.20.27॥

Word-by-word meaning: वन-ओकसः the inhabitants of the forest (birds, animals, etc.), प्रमुदिताः were greatly delighted, वन-राजीः the rows of forest trees, मधु-च्युतः dripping with honey/sweet sap, जल-धाराः the waterfalls, गिरेः from the mountain, नादान् sounds, आसन्नाः nearby, ददृशे saw (or "were observed"), गुहाः the caves

Anvaya: प्रमुदिताः वनौकसः, मधुच्युतः वनराजीः, गिरेः जलधाराः, नादान् (च) आसन्नाः गुहाः (सः हरिः) ददृशे।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: प्रमुदिताः वनौकसः The greatly delighted inhabitants of the forest, मधुच्युतः वनराजीः the rows of trees dripping with sweet honey, जलधाराः the torrents of water, गिरेः नादान् (च) and the resounding sounds from the mountain, आसन्नाः गुहाः (in) the nearby caves, (सः हरिः) ददृशे (Lord Hari) observed.

Simple meaning: Lord Krishna observed the joyful residents of the forest—the birds and animals—and the long rows of trees that were literally dripping with honey and sweet sap. He saw the waterfalls cascading from the mountainside and heard the deep, echoing sounds of the water splashing into the nearby caves.

The Sensory Experience of Vrindavana

This verse transitions from the cows to the broader landscape of the forest, appealing to all senses:

  • Sight: The rows of trees (वनराजीः) and waterfalls (जलधाराः).

  • Taste/Smell: The dripping honey (मधुच्युतः) which signifies the absolute peak of abundance.

  • Sound: The roaring of waterfalls and the unique acoustics of the mountain caves

  • Emotion: The general feeling is one of प्रमुदिताः —intense, spiritual delight shared by all living beings in the presence of the Lord.

क्वचित् वनस्पतिक्रोडे गुहायां चाभिवर्षति ।

निर्विश्य भगवान् रेमे कन्दमूलफलाशनः॥10.20.28॥

Word-by-word meaning: क्वचित् sometimes, वनस्पति-क्रोडे in the hollow (lap) of a great tree, गुहायाम् in a cave, च and, अभिवर्षति when it was raining heavily, निर्विश्य having entered, भगवान् the Supreme Lord, रेमे enjoyed/sported, कन्द-मूल-फल-अशनः eating bulbs, roots, and fruits

Anvaya: क्वचित् अभिवर्षति (सति) भगवान् वनस्पतिक्रोडे गुहायां च निर्विश्य कन्दमूलफलाशनः (सन्) रेमे।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: क्वचित् Sometimes, अभिवर्षति (सति) when it was raining heavily, भगवान् the Supreme Lord, वनस्पतिक्रोडे in the hollow of a tree, गुहायां च and in a cave, निर्विश्य having entered, कन्दमूलफलाशनः (सन्) while eating bulbs, roots, and fruits, रेमे enjoyed.

Simple meaning: Sometimes, when the rain came down in torrents, the Supreme Lord Krishna would take shelter inside the hollow of a massive tree or within a mountain cave. There, He enjoyed Himself simply, sporting with His friends and eating the various bulbs, roots, and fruits found in the forest.

दध्योदनं समानीतं शिलायां सलिलान्तिके ।

सम्भोजनीयैर्बुभुजे गोपैः सङ्कर्षणान्वितः ॥10.20.29॥

Word-by-word meaning: दधि-ओदनम् rice mixed with yogurt (curd-rice), समानीतम् brought (from home), शिलायाम् on a stone slab, सलिल-अन्तिके near the water, सम्भोजनीयैः who were worthy of eating together, बुभुजे ate, गोपैः with the cowherd boys, सङ्कर्षण-अन्वितः accompanied by Lord Balarama (Sankarshana)

Anvaya: सलिलान्तिके शिलायां सङ्कर्षणान्वितः (सः) गोपैः (सह) समानीतं सम्भोजनीयैः दध्योदनं बुभुजे।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: सलिलान्तिके शिलायां On a stone slab near the water, सङ्कर्षणान्वितः (सः) He (Krishna), accompanied by Balarama, गोपैः (सह) together with the cowherd boys, समानीतं सम्भोजनीयैः दध्योदनं the curd-rice brought (from home), together with the accompaniments (like vegetables, pickles etc), बुभुजे ate.

Simple meaning: Sitting on a large stone slab near the water's edge, Lord Krishna and Balarama along with the cowherd boys shared the curd-rice together with the accompaniments (like vegetables, pickles etc), they had brought from home.

शाद्वलोपरि संविश्य चर्वतो मीलितेक्षणान् ।

तृप्तान् वृषान् वत्सतरान् गाश्च स्वोधोभरश्रमाः ॥10.20.30॥

Word-by-word meaning: शाद्वल-उपरि upon the lush green grass, संविश्य sitting down, चर्वतः chewing (the cud), मीलित-ईक्षणान् with eyes closed, तृप्तान् satisfied, वृषान् the bulls, वत्सतरान् the calves, गाः the cows, च and, स्व-ऊधः-भर-श्रमाः fatigued by the weight of their own udders

Anvaya: शाद्वलोपरि संविश्य चर्वतः मीलितेक्षणान् तृप्तान् वृषान् वत्सतरान् स्वोधोभरश्रमाः गाः च (कृष्णः ददृशे)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: शाद्वलोपरि संविश्य Sitting upon the green grass, चर्वतः chewing their cud, मीलितेक्षणान् with their eyes closed, तृप्तान् वृषान् वत्सतरान् स्वोधोभरश्रमाः गाः च the satisfied bulls, the calves, and the cows—who were fatigued by the weight of their own udders — (कृष्णः ददृशे) (Lord Krishna saw)

Simple meaning: Lord Krishna looked upon the scene of peaceful abundance: the bulls, calves, and heavy-uddered cows were resting comfortably on the soft, green forest floor. Having eaten their fill, they sat contentedly, eyes closed in relaxation, slowly chewing their cud.

प्रावृट्श्रियं च तां वीक्ष्य सर्वभूतमुदावहाम् ।

भगवान् पूजयाञ्चक्रे आत्मशक्त्युपबृंहिताम् ॥10.20.31॥

Word-by-word meaning: प्रावृट्-श्रियम् the beauty/opulence of the rainy season, च and, ताम् that, वीक्ष्य having observed, सर्व-भूत-मुद-आवहाम् bringing joy to all living beings, भगवान् the Supreme Lord, पूजयाञ्चक्रे honored/glorified, आत्म-शक्ति-उपबृंहिताम् expanded by His own internal potencies

Anvaya: सर्वभूतमुदावहाम् आत्मशक्त्युपबृंहितां च तां प्रावृट्श्रियं वीक्ष्य भगवान् पूजयाञ्चक्रे।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: सर्वभूतमुदावहाम् आत्मशक्त्युपबृंहितां च Bringing joy to all living beings and expanded by His own internal energy, तां प्रावृट्श्रियं having observed that beauty of the rainy season, भगवान् पूजयाञ्चक्रे the Supreme Lord honored it.

Simple meaning: Observing the magnificent beauty of the monsoon season—which brought delight to every living creature and was actually a manifestation of His own internal creative energy—the Supreme Lord Krishna felt great satisfaction and expressed His appreciation for its splendor.

The Creator and His Creation

This verse provides a deep theological conclusion to the rainy season section:

  • आत्म-शक्ति-उपबृंहिताम्: This confirms that the beauty Krishna was enjoying was not "material" in the ordinary sense, it was an expansion of His own आत्म-शक्ति (internal potency). The forest of Vrindavana is an extension of the Lord’s own being.

  • पूजयाञ्चक्रे: This is a fascinating choice of word. It suggests that the Lord "worshiped" or "honored" the season. It shows the Lord's humility and His delight in seeing His own energy provide happiness (मुद-आवहाम्) to all the bhutas (living beings)

एवं निवसतोस्तस्मिन् रामकेशवयोर्व्रजे ।

शरत् समभवत् व्यभ्रा स्वच्छाम्ब्वपरुषानिला ॥10.20.32॥

Word-by-word meaning: एवम् in this way, निवसतोः while they were dwelling, तस्मिन् in that, राम-केशवयोः of Balarama and Krishna, व्रजे in the cowherd settlement (Vraja), शरत् the autumn season, समभवत् appeared, व्यभ्रा cloudless, स्वच्छ-अम्बु-अ-परुष-अनिला with clear water and gentle breezes

Anvaya: तस्मिन् व्रजे रामकेशवयोः एवं निवसतोः व्यभ्रा स्वच्छाम्ब्वपरुषानिला शरत् समभवत्।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: तस्मिन् In that settlement, रामकेशवयोः एवं निवसतोः Balarama and Krishna, in this way, while dwelling, व्यभ्रा स्वच्छाम्ब्वपरुषानिला cloudless and having clear water and non-harsh breezes, शरत् समभवत् Autumn appeared.

Simple meaning: While Balarama and Krishna were living in Vraja in this way, the autumn season began. The sky became free of clouds, the water turned crystal clear, and the winds became gentle and refreshing.

शरदा नीरजोत्पत्त्या नीराणि प्रकृतिं ययुः।

भ्रष्टानामिव चेतांसि पुनर्योगनिषेवया ॥10.20.33॥

Word-by-word meaning: शरदा by the autumn season, नीरज-उत्पत्त्या by the birth of lotuses, नीराणि the waters, प्रकृतिम् to their natural (original) state, ययुः went/returned, भ्रष्टानाम् of those who have fallen (from their path), इव like, चेतांसि the minds, पुनः again, योग-निषेवया by the practice of yoga/devotional service

Anvaya: शरदा नीरजोत्पत्त्या नीराणि – योगनिषेवया पुनः भ्रष्टानां चेतांसि इव – प्रकृतिं ययुः।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: नीरजोत्पत्त्या शरदा Autumn marked with the birth of lotuses, नीराणि the waters, योगनिषेवया इव just as by the practice of yoga, पुनः again, भ्रष्टानां चेतांसि the minds of the fallen, प्रकृतिं ययुः returned to their natural state.

Simple meaning: The arrival of autumn marked with the birth of many lotuses, caused the waters of the lakes, ponds and rivers to return to their natural state of clarity and purity. This is just like the minds of people who have fallen from their spiritual path, when they take up the practice of yoga or devotional service again, their consciousness returns to its original, pure state.

Philosophical Comparison

  • The Muddy Water: Represents the mind when it is agitated by material desires and the "storms" of life (the rainy season).

  • The Lotuses (नीरज): Represent the blossoming of spiritual knowledge or the presence of the Lord. The word नीरज (born from water) specifically points to the lotus, which remains in the water but is untouched by it.

  • The Restoration (प्रकृतिम्): In Sanskrit, प्रकृति can mean "original nature." The verse suggests that purity is the water's original state, just as peace and clarity are the soul's original state.

  • योगनिषेवया : The practice of yoga is the "autumn" for the soul—it settles the "mud" of worldly attachment and allows the natural beauty of the self to shine through once more.

व्योम्नोऽब्दं भूतशाबल्यं भुवः पङ्कमपां मलम् ।

शरञ्जहाराश्रमिणां कृष्णे भक्तिर्यथाशुभम् ॥10.20.34॥

Word-by-word meaning: व्योम्नः from the sky, अब्दम् the clouds, भूत-शाबल्यम् the variegatedness of living beings (insects/vegetation), भुवः from the earth, पङ्कम् the mud, अपाम् of the waters, मलम् the turbidity/impurity, शरत् autumn, जहार removed/took away, आश्रमिणाम् of those in the four ashramas (stages of life), कृष्णे for Krishna, भक्तिः devotional service, यथा just as, अशुभम् inauspiciousness/sins

Anvaya: शरत् व्योम्नः अब्दं, भुवः पङ्कम्, अपां मलं, भूतशाबल्यं (च) जहार, यथा कृष्णे भक्तिः आश्रमिणाम् अशुभं (जहाति)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: शरत् Autumn, व्योम्नः अब्दं from the sky, the clouds, भुवः पङ्कम् from the earth, the mud, अपां मलं the impurity of the waters, भूतशाबल्यं (च) and the crowding/variegatedness of living beings, जहार took away, यथा just as, कृष्णे भक्तिः devotion in Krishna, आश्रमिणाम् of those in the four stages of life, अशुभम् inauspiciousness (takes away).

Simple meaning: Autumn removed the clouds from the sky, the mud from the earth, the turbidity from the water, and the excessive crowding of various insects and plants. In the same way, devotional service to Lord Krishna removes all inauspiciousness and sinful impurities from the hearts of those living in any of the four stages of life.

The Cleansing Power of Autumn

The poet lists four specific "impurities" of the rainy season that Autumn cleanses, creating a parallel to spiritual purification when the mind is devoted to Krishna:

  • The Sky (Sky/Clouds): Represents the removal of mental obscuration.

  • The Earth (Mud): Represents the removal of physical obstacles or "clinging" habits.

  • The Water (Turbidity): Represents the clarity of consciousness.

  • भूतशाबल्यम्: This refers to the disappearance of the swarms of insects and weeds that flourished during the monsoon. Spiritually, this represents the clearing of the "clutter" of secondary desires.

Just as the sun of Autumn naturally dries the mud and clears the sky, Bhakti naturally dissolves the अशुभम् (sins or misfortune) of the practitioner without requiring separate arduous efforts.

सर्वस्वं जलदा हित्वा विरेजुः शुभ्रवर्चसः ।

यथा त्यक्तैषणाः शान्ता मुनयो मुक्तकिल्बिषा ॥10.20.35॥

Word-by-word meaning: सर्वस्वम् everything (their water), जल-दाः the clouds (lit. "water-givers"), हित्वा having given up, विरेजुः shone brilliantly, शुभ्र-वर्चसः of white/pure luster, यथा just as, त्यक्त-एषणाः those who have given up all desires, शान्ताः peaceful, मुनयः sages, मुक्त-किल्बिषाः freed from all sins

Anvaya: जलदाः सर्वस्वं हित्वा शुभ्रवर्चसः (सन्तः) विरेजुः, यथा त्यक्तैषणाः मुक्तकिल्बिषाः शान्ताः मुनयः (विराजन्ते)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: जलदाः सर्वस्वं हित्वा The clouds having given up everything (their water), शुभ्रवर्चसः of white luster, विरेजुः shone, यथा just as, त्यक्तैषणाः those who have given up desires, मुक्तकिल्बिषाः freed from sins, शान्ताः मुनयः peaceful sages (shine).

Simple meaning: Having given away their entire wealth of water, the clouds became weightless and pure white, shining with a brilliant luster. They resembled peaceful sages who, having renounced all worldly desires and being freed from the stain of sin, shine with the inner light of spiritual peace.

Philosophical Comparison

  • Giving up "Everything": The clouds are called जलदाः (givers of water). By exhausting their supply for the benefit of the earth, they become "white" (शुभ्र). This represents the paradox of renunciation: by giving everything up, one becomes light and radiant.

  • त्यक्तैषणाः : This refers to the three primary worldly desires (wealth, progeny, and fame). When a sage (muni) lets these go, the "darkness" of attachment leaves his heart, just as the dark water leaves the cloud.

  • शान्ताः : Real peace comes only after the "storm" of activity and the "burden" of possession have passed. The white, autumn clouds drifting effortlessly represent the liberated soul (मुक्त-किल्बिषाः) moving through the world without being anchored by gravity or sin.

गिरयो मुमुचुस्तोयं क्वचिन्न मुमुचुः शिवम् ।

यथा ज्ञानामृतं काले ज्ञानिनो ददते न वा ॥10.20.36॥

Word-by-word meaning: गिरयः the mountains, मुमुचुः released, तोयम् water, क्वचित् in some places, न not, मुमुचुः released, शिवम् pure/auspicious, यथा just as, ज्ञान-अमृतम् the nectar of knowledge, काले at the appropriate time, ज्ञानिनः the realized souls/sages, ददते they give, न वा or not

Anvaya: गिरयः क्वचित् शिवं तोयं मुमुचुः, क्वचित् न मुमुचुः, यथा काले ज्ञानिनः ज्ञानामृतं ददते न वा (तथा)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: गिरयः The mountains, क्वचित् in some places, शिवम् pure/auspicious, तोयम् water, मुमुचुः released, (च) and, क्वचित् न मुमुचुः in some places did not release, यथा just as, काले at the appropriate time, ज्ञानिनः the realized souls, ज्ञानामृतम् the nectar of knowledge, ददते give, न वा or do not.

Simple meaning: In the autumn, some waterfalls on the mountains continued to pour down pure, auspicious water, while others stopped flowing. This is just like realized sages, who sometimes bestow the nectar of their spiritual wisdom upon others when the time and recipient are right, and at other times remain silent and withdrawn in their own realization.

Philosophical Comparison

  • The Mountain Waterfalls: Represent the flow of realized knowledge (ज्ञान). During the rains, every crack in the mountain flowed with water, but in autumn, only the deep and steady sources remain active.

  • The Sages (ज्ञानिनः): They do not give knowledge indiscriminately. Like the autumn mountain, they are selective and act according to the काल (time) and the qualification of the seeker.

  • शिवम्: This implies that the water—and the knowledge—is not just informative, but transformative and "auspicious" for the world.

  • न वा (Or Not): This highlights the independence (svatantrya) of a self-realized soul. They are not compelled by social pressure to speak, they share their "nectar" only when it will truly benefit the "parched" souls of the world.

नैवाविदन् क्षीयमाणं जलं गाधजलेचराः ।

यथाऽऽयुरन्वहं क्षय्यं नरा मूढाः कुटुम्बिनः ॥10.20.37॥

Word-by-word meaning: न not, एव indeed, अविदन् realized/knew, क्षीयमाणम् diminishing, जलम् water, गाध-जल-चराः those living in shallow water (the fish), यथा just as, आयुः life span, अन्वहम् day by day, क्षय्यम् being exhausted, नराः people, मूढाः foolish, कुटुम्बिनः householders attached to family

Anvaya: गाधजलेचराः क्षीयमाणं जलं न एव अविदन्, यथा अन्वहं क्षय्यं आयुः मूढाः कुटुम्बिनः नराः (न विदन्ति)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: गाधजलेचराः Those moving in shallow water, क्षीयमाणं जलं diminishing water, न एव अविदन् did not at all realize, यथा just as, अन्वहं day by day, क्षय्यं being exhausted, आयुः life span, मूढाः कुटुम्बिनः नराः foolish people attached to family (do not realize).

Simple meaning: The fish living in the shallowing waters of autumn did not realize that their habitat was drying up day by day. In the same way, foolish householders who are overly absorbed in domestic affairs do not realize that their life span is being exhausted with every passing day.

Philosophical Comparison

  • The Shallow Water (गाध-जल): This refers to the ponds and puddles that were filled during the rain but are now evaporating in the autumn sun. It represents the temporary and fragile nature of material security.

  • The Fish (जले-चराः): Like the fish who continue to play and compete for space in a shrinking pond, people often focus on small, temporary gains while the "water" of their life is disappearing.

  • मूढाः कुटुम्बिनः : The verse specifically targets the "foolish householder" not because household life is inherently bad, but because of the delusion (moha) that life is permanent.

  • अन्वहं क्षय्यं: This emphasizes the relentless, incremental nature of time. Just as a pool doesn't dry up all at once, life is not lost at the moment of death—it is being "spent" every single day.

गाधवारिचरास्तापमविन्दञ्छरदर्कजम् ।

यथा दरिद्रः कृपणः कुटुम्ब्यविजितेन्द्रियः ॥10.20.38॥

Word-by-word meaning: गाध-वारि-चराः those living in shallow water, तापम् distress/heat, अविन्दन् experienced, शरद्-अर्क-जम् born from the autumn sun, यथा just as, दरिद्रः a poor man, कृपणः miserable/wretched, कुटुम्बी a householder, अविजित-इन्द्रियः whose senses are uncontrolled

Anvaya: गाधवारिचराः शरदर्केजं तापम् अविन्दन्, यथा अविजितेन्द्रियः दरिद्रः कृपणः कुटुम्बी (तापम् अविन्दति)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: गाधवारिचराः Those living in shallow water, शरदर्केजं born from the autumn sun, तापम् अविन्दन् experienced distress, यथा just as, अविजितेन्द्रियः one whose senses are not conquered, दरिद्रः कृपणः कुटुम्बी poor wretched householder (experiences distress).

Simple meaning: The creatures living in shallow water suffered greatly from the scorching heat of the autumn sun. They were like a poor, wretched householder who, having failed to control his senses, is constantly overwhelmed by the burning anxieties and miseries.

Philosophical Comparison

  • The Autumn Sun (शरद्-अर्क): While the autumn sun is pleasant to some, it is deadly to those in shallow water. It represents the "heat" of material consequences and time.

  • Shallow Water: Represents a lack of spiritual depth or merit. Without the "depth" of wisdom, one is easily scorched by the trials of life.

  • The Uncontrolled Householder: The distress of the fish is compared to a person who is both financially poor (दरिद्रः) and spiritually "poor" because he cannot control his desires (अविजितेन्द्रियः). Such a person is constantly "burned" by the needs and demands of his family and senses, foundering in the "shallow" pool of material existence.

शनै शनैर्जहुः पङ्कं स्थलान्यामं च वीरुधः ।

यथाहंममतां धीराः शरीरादिष्वनात्मसु ॥10.20.39॥

Word-by-word meaning: शनैः शनैः gradually, जहुः gave up/abandoned, पङ्कम् the mud, स्थलानि the dry lands, आमम् the rawness/unripeness, च and, वीरुधः the plants/creepers, यथा just as, अहम्-ममताम् "I-ness" and "my-ness" (ego and possessiveness), धीराः the wise/sober, शरीर-आदिषु in the body and so on, अन्-आत्मसु in things that are not the self.

Anvaya: स्थलानि शनैः शनैः पङ्कं जहुः, वीरुधः (च) आमं (जहुः), यथा धीराः अनात्मसु शरीरादिषु अहंममतां (त्यजन्ति)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: स्थलानि The dry lands, शनैः शनैः gradually, पङ्कं जहुः gave up mud, वीरुधः (च) and the plants, आमं (जहुः) gave up rawness, यथा just as, धीराः the wise, अनात्मसु in the non-self, शरीरादिषु in the body and so on, अहंममतां ego and possessiveness (give up).

Simple meaning: Little by little, the land gave up its mud and became dry, and the plants gave up their rawness as they reached maturity. In the same way, wise and sober people gradually give up the sense of "I" and "mine" regarding the physical body and other material things which are not the true self.

The Process of Ripening

This verse beautifully links the physical drying of the earth to the spiritual maturation of a seeker:

  • शनैः शनैः : This underscores that both nature and spiritual growth happen "gradually." Just as the earth cannot dry instantly without cracking, the ego is shed through steady practice, not usually in a single moment.

  • आमं (Rawness): This refers to the acidic, unripe state of plants. As they ripen, they become sweet. Similarly, as a person sheds "I-ness" (अहम्) and "my-ness" (ममता), their character loses its "sour" material aggression and becomes "sweet" with spiritual wisdom.

  • अनात्मसु : The body, home, and possessions are labeled "non-self." The धीराः (sober person) is one who recognizes that while these are useful, they are not the essence of his identity.

निश्चलाम्बुरभूत्तूष्णीं समुद्रः शरदागमे ।

आत्मन्युपरते सम्यङ्मुनिर्व्युपरतागमः ॥10.20.40॥

Word-by-word meaning: निश्चल-अम्बुः whose waters are still, अभूत् became, तूष्णीम् silent, समुद्रः the ocean, शरद्-आगमे with the arrival of autumn, आत्मनि in the self, उपरते when ceased (from worldly activity), सम्यक् completely, मुनिः a sage, व्युपरत-आगमः whose study of scriptures (or ritualistic noise) has ceased

Anvaya: शरदागमे समुद्रः निश्चलाम्बुः तूष्णीं (च) अभूत्, यथा आत्मनि सम्यक् उपरते व्युपरतागमः मुनिः (तूष्णीं भवति)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: शरदागमे With the arrival of autumn, समुद्रः the ocean, निश्चलाम्बुः with still waters, तूष्णीम् अभूत् became silent, यथा just as, आत्मनि in the self, सम्यक् completely, उपरते when withdrawn, व्युपरतागमः one whose scriptural study/ritual has ceased, मुनिः a sage (becomes silent).

Simple meaning: With the arrival of autumn, the ocean’s waters became perfectly still and its roaring waves fell silent. It resembled a sage who has completely withdrawn his mind from worldly affairs and, having realized the Self, has ceased even the external study of scriptures or the performance of rituals, remaining in a state of profound, silent peace.

The Silence of Realization

The monsoon ocean is characterized by turbulent waves and loud roaring, but the autumn ocean represents the state of Samadhi:

  • निश्चल-अम्बुः : During the rains, the rivers poured agitated water into the sea. Now, as the inflow stops, the ocean becomes "still." This represents a mind no longer agitated by the "rivers" of sensory input.

  • तूष्णीम् : The silence of the ocean is the silence of depth.

  • व्युपरत-आगमः : This is a profound philosophical point. आगमः refers to the Vedas or external scriptures. For a sage who has "arrived" at the direct experience of the Self (आत्मनि), the need for external pointers or loud recitations ceases. He moves from "talking about" the truth to "being" the truth.

केदारेभ्यस्त्वपोऽगृह्णन् कर्षका दृढसेतुभिः ।

यथा प्राणैः स्रवज्ज्ञानं तन्निरोधेन योगिनः ॥10.20.41॥

Word-by-word meaning: केदारेभ्यः from the agricultural fields, तु indeed, अपः the water, अगृह्णन् held back/restrained, कर्षकाः the farmers, दृढ-सेतुभिः with strong embankments/dikes, यथा just as, प्राणैः through the senses, स्रवत् flowing out, ज्ञानम् knowledge/consciousness, तत्-निरोधेन by their restraint, योगिनः the yogis

Anvaya: कर्षकाः दृढसेतुभिः केदारेभ्यः अपः अगृह्णन्, यथा योगिनः तन्निरोधेन प्राणैः स्रवज्ज्ञानं (गृह्णन्ति)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: कर्षकाः The farmers, दृढसेतुभिः with strong dikes, केदारेभ्यः from the fields, अपः the water, अगृह्णन् held back, यथा just as, योगिनः yogis, तन्निरोधेन by their (senses') restraint, प्राणैः through the senses, स्रवज्ज्ञानं flowing knowledge/energy (restrain).

Simple meaning: Just as farmers build strong dikes to prevent the water from flowing out of their fields, yogis practice the restraint of their senses and breath to prevent their spiritual energy and knowledge from leaking away into the material world.

The Conservation of Energy

This verse uses a practical agricultural metaphor to explain a subtle yogic principle:

  • The Water (अपः): Water is the lifeblood of the field. If it leaks out, the crop dies. In spiritual life, the "crop" is realization, and the "water" is the vital energy and focus of the soul.

  • The Senses (प्राणैः): Here, प्राण refers to the outgoing senses. Naturally, our attention "flows out" toward sights, sounds, and desires.

  • Nirodhena: This mirrors Patanjali’s definition of Yoga (citta-vritti-nirodha). By building "strong dikes" (दृढ-सेतुभिः) of discipline and meditation, the yogi ensures that their consciousness remains concentrated within.

शरदर्कांशुजांस्तापान् भूतानामुडुपोऽहरत् ।

देहाभिमानजं बोधो मुकुन्दो व्रजयोषिताम् ॥10.20.42॥

Word-by-word meaning: शरद्-अर्क-अंशु-जान् born from the rays of the autumn sun, तापान् the sufferings/heat, भूतानाम् of all living beings, उडुपः the moon (lit. "lord of the stars"), अहरत् removed, देह-अभिमान-जम् born from identification with the body, बोधः spiritual knowledge, मुकुन्दः Lord Mukunda (Krishna), व्रज-योषिताम् of the women of Vraja

Anvaya: उडुपः भूतानां शरदर्कांशुजान् तापान् अहरत्, यथा बोधः देहाभिमानजं (तापं हरति), (यथा च) मुकुन्दः व्रजयोषितां (तापम् अहरत्)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: उडुपः The moon, भूतानां of living beings, शरदर्कांशुजान् तापान् अहरत् removed the heat born from the rays of the autumn sun, यथा just as, बोधः spiritual knowledge, देहाभिमानजं born from bodily identification (removes heat), (यथा च) and just as, मुकुन्दः Lord Mukunda, व्रजयोषिताम् of the women of Vraja (removed the heat).

Simple meaning: The autumn moon removed the distress caused to all living beings by the burning rays of the autumn sun. This is similar to how spiritual knowledge removes the suffering born from bodily identification, and just as Lord Mukunda removed the burning ache of separation from the gopis of Vraja.

The Cooling Grace

This verse contains a "double" comparison, linking a natural phenomenon to both a philosophical truth and a specific narrative emotion:

  • The Moon (उडुपः): In Sanskrit poetry, the moon is the "physician" of the night, offering cooling rays to heal the burns of the day.

  • बोधः vs. देहाभिमान : Philosophical suffering is compared to "heat" (ताप). The root cause is देह-अभिमान (the false ego). बोधः (awakening/knowledge) acts as the cool moonlight that dissolves this fever.

  • Mukunda and the Gopis: This is a poignant transition. For the women of Vraja, their "heat" was the intense longing (viraha) for Krishna. Krishna's presence (as Mukunda, the giver of liberation/bliss) is the ultimate moonlight that satisfies their hearts.

खमशोभत निर्मेघं शरद्विमलतारकम् ।

सत्त्वयुक्तं यथा चित्तं शब्दब्रह्मार्थदर्शनम् ॥10.20.43॥

Word-by-word meaning: खम् the sky, अशोभत shone/became beautiful, निर्मेघम् free from clouds, शरद्-विमल-तारकम् with the clear stars of autumn, सत्त्व-युक्तम् endowed with the quality of goodness (sattva), यथा just as, चित्तम् the consciousness/mind, शब्द-ब्रह्म-अर्थ-दर्शनम् perceive the meaning of the Vedic scriptures (shabda-brahma)

Anvaya: शरद्विमलतारकं निर्मेघं खम् अशोभत, यथा शब्दब्रह्मार्थदर्शनं सत्त्वयुक्तं चित्तं (शोभते)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: शरद्विमलतारकं Possessing the clear stars of autumn, निर्मेघं free from clouds, खम् the sky, अशोभत shone, यथा just as, शब्दब्रह्मार्थदर्शनं perceiving the meaning of the Vedic scriptures, सत्त्वयुक्तं endowed with goodness, चित्तम् the mind (shines).

Simple meaning: The autumn sky, now free from clouds and filled with brilliantly clear stars, shone with great beauty. It was like a mind purified by perceiving the true, deep meaning of the Vedic scriptures.

Clarity of Vision

This verse draws a parallel between the macrocosm of the sky and the microcosm of human consciousness:

  • निर्मेघम् (Cloudless): In the rainy season, the sky was covered, hiding the stars. This represents the mind covered by Rajas (passion) and Tamas (ignorance). When the "clouds" are removed, the "stars" of truth become visible.

  • शब्द-ब्रह्म-अर्थ-दर्शनम्: The Vedas are called Shabda-brahma (Brahman in the form of sound). Their true "meaning" (artha) isn't found through mere intellectual study, but through darshanam (direct perception).

  • सत्त्व-युक्तम्: Clarity is not an act of will, but a result of purity. Just as the stars are always there but only seen when the sky is clear, spiritual truths are always present but only perceived when the Chitta (consciousness) is established in Sattva.

अखण्डमण्डलो व्योम्नि रराजोडुगणैः शशी ।

यथा यदुपतिः कृष्णो वृष्णिचक्रावृतो भुवि ॥10.20.44॥

Word-by-word meaning: अखण्ड-मण्डलः possessing a full/perfect orb, व्योम्नि in the sky, रराज shone, उडु-गणैः with the hosts of stars, शशी the moon, यथा just as, यदु-पतिः the Lord of the Yadus, कृष्णः Krishna, वृष्णि-चक्र-आवृतः surrounded by the circle of the Vrishnis, भुवि on the earth

Anvaya: उडुगणैः (सह) अखण्डमण्डलः शशी व्योम्नि रराज, यथा वृष्णिचक्रावृतः यदुपतिः कृष्णः भुवि (राजते)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: उडुगणैः With the hosts of stars, अखण्डमण्डलः शशी the moon having a full orb, व्योम्नि रराज shone in the sky, यथा just as, वृष्णिचक्रावृतः surrounded by the circle of the Vrishnis, यदुपतिः कृष्णः the Lord of the Yadus, Krishna, भुवि on the earth (shines).

Simple meaning: The full moon, with its perfect circular orb, shone brilliantly in the sky surrounded by countless stars. It appeared just like Lord Krishna, the master of the Yadus, when He appeared on earth surrounded by the circle of His Vrishni dynasty.

The King and the Moon

This verse provides a beautiful visual parallel that connects the celestial realm to the earthly pastimes of the Lord:

  • अखण्ड-मण्डलः : The "unbroken circle" refers to the full moon of the autumn season. This fullness represents perfection and complete beauty.

  • वृष्णि-चक्र : The Vrishni dynasty members are compared to the stars. Just as stars derive their light from the moon and enhance its beauty, the members of the Vrishni clan are the "extensions" of Krishna’s own glory.

  • यदुपतिः : By calling Him the "Lord of the Yadus," the poet emphasizes His role as the central, gravity-defying point around which all His devotees revolve, much like the stars revolve around the moon in the night sky.

आश्लिष्य समशीतोष्णं प्रसूनवनमारुतम् ।

जनास्तापं जहुर्गोप्यो न कृष्णहृतचेतसः ॥10.20.45॥

Word-by-word meaning: आश्लिष्य having embraced, सम-शीत-उष्णम् neither too cold nor too hot (temperate), प्रसून-वन-मारुतम् the breeze from the flowery forests, जनाः the people, तापम् their distress/fatigue, जहुः gave up/forgot, गोप्यः the gopis, न not, कृष्ण-हृत-चेतसः those whose hearts were stolen by Krishna

Anvaya: समशीतोष्णं प्रसूनवनमारुतम् आश्लिष्य जनाः तापं जहुः, (परन्तु) कृष्णहृतचेतसः गोप्यः न (जहुः)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: समशीतोष्णं प्रसूनवनमारुतम् Temperate (Not too cold and hot) breeze from the flowering forests, आश्लिष्य having embraced, जनाः the people, तापं जहुः gave up distress/fatigue, (परन्तु) but, कृष्णहृतचेतसः those whose hearts were stolen by Krishna, गोप्यः the gopis, न did not (give up their distress).

Simple meaning: By embracing the temperate autumn breeze that carried the scent of blossoming forests, the people of Vraja found relief from all their physical fatigue. However, the gopis, whose hearts had been stolen by Krishna, could not find relief from the burning ache of their love for Him.

The Contrast of Relief

This verse highlights the difference between physical comfort and the "divine distress" of love:

  • सम-शीत-उष्णम्: The autumn breeze is perfect—it is neither biting cold nor scorching hot. It is the ideal physical environment for comfort.

  • जहुः vs. न : For the average person (जनाः), external conditions dictate internal state. A pleasant breeze removes their ताप (heat/misery). But for the gopis, their "heat" is internal.

  • कृष्ण-हृत-चेतसः : This is the technical reason for their lack of relief. Since Krishna has "stolen" their hearts/minds, they no longer belong to themselves. Physical comforts cannot soothe a heart that isn't there, only the return of the "thief" (Krishna) can cure their longing.

गावो मृगा खगा नार्यः पुष्पिण्यः शरदाभवन् ।

अन्वीयमानाः स्ववृषैः फलैरीशक्रिया इव ॥10.20.46॥

Word-by-word meaning: गावः the cows, मृगाः the deer, खगाः the birds, नार्यः the women, पुष्पिण्यः fertile/blooming (in their menses), शरदा by the autumn season, अभवन् became, अन्वीयमानाः being followed, स्व-वृषैः by their respective males, फलैः by results, ईश-क्रियाः activities performed for the Lord, इव like

Anvaya: शरदा स्ववृषैः अन्वीयमानाः गावः मृगाः खगाः नार्यः (च) पुष्पिण्यः अभवन्, फलैः ईशक्रियाः इव।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: शरदा In autumn, स्ववृषैः अन्वीयमानाः being followed by their own males, गावः मृगाः खगाः नार्यः the cows, the deer, the birds and the women, पुष्पिण्यः became fertile/blooming, फलैः ईशक्रियाः इव just as results (follow) the activities performed for the Lord.

Simple meaning: Under the influence of autumn, followed by their respective mates the cows, deer, birds, and women all conceived. Just as selfless activities performed for the Supreme Lord are naturally and inevitably followed by their auspicious results.

The Law of Natural Consequence

This verse uses a striking metaphor to link biological fertility with spiritual merit:

  • पुष्पिण्यः : In a literal sense, this refers to blooming flowers, but here it specifically refers to the fertility cycle of living beings. Autumn is seen as the season of fruition.

  • स्व-वृषैः : The "males" (bulls, stags, etc.) follow the females by natural instinct.

  • ईश-क्रियाः : This is the most significant part of the verse. It refers to Nishkama-karma or acts dedicated to God.

  • The Comparison: Just as a male animal is naturally drawn to a fertile female, and just as a flower is naturally followed by fruit, a deed done for Krishna must result in a spiritual blessing. The "fruit" (phala) of such actions is not a matter of chance, it is a law of spiritual nature as certain as the seasons.

उदहृष्यन् वारिजानि सूर्योत्थाने कुमुद् विना ।

राज्ञा तु निर्भया लोका यथा दस्यून् विना नृप ॥10.20.47॥

Word-by-word meaning: उदहृष्यन् rejoiced/bloomed, वारिजानि the water-born flowers (lotuses), सूर्य-उत्थाने upon the rising of the sun, कुमुद् the night-blooming lily, विना except, राज्ञा by a (good) king, तु indeed, निर्भयाः fearless, लोकाः the people, यथा just as, दस्यून् thieves/rogues, विना without, नृप O King (Parikshit)

Anvaya: नृप! सूर्योत्थाने कुमुद् विना वारिजानि उदहृष्यन्, यथा राज्ञा (सति) दस्यून् विना लोकाः निर्भयाः (भवन्ति)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: नृप O King, सूर्योत्थाने upon the rising of the sun, कुमुद् विना except the night-blooming lily, वारिजानि the water-born lotuses, उदहृष्यन् bloomed/rejoiced, यथा just as, राज्ञा by a king, दस्यून् विना without thieves, लोकाः निर्भयाः the people (become) fearless .

Simple meaning: O King Parikshit, when the sun rose, all the water-born lotuses bloomed with joy, except for the night-blooming lily. Similarly, under the rule of a capable king, all citizens become fearless and thrive, with the sole exception of thieves and rogues, who find no joy in a well-governed state.

The Sun and the Lotus

This verse uses a classic poetic device to illustrate the effects of righteous leadership:

  • The Sun as the King: The sun provides the light and energy necessary for the lotuses to "rejoice" (उदहृष्यन्). Likewise, a good king provides the security and order necessary for society to flourish.

  • The Kumud (Night Lily): The kumuda flower blooms only at night and closes when the sun rises. In this metaphor, it represents the दस्यू (thieves or criminals).

  • Fearlessness (निर्भयाः): The rising of the sun makes the world safe for the lotus but makes the "thief" hide. A righteous administration is a "sunrise" for the law-abiding citizen but a source of dread for those who live in the shadows of crime.

पुरग्रामेष्वाग्रयणैरैन्द्रियैश्च महोत्सवैः ।

बभौ भूः पक्वसस्याढ्या कलाभ्यां नितरां हरेः ॥10.20.48॥

Word-by-word meaning: पुर-ग्रामेषु in the cities and villages, आग्रयणैः with the Agrayana sacrifices (offering of the first grains), ऐन्द्रियैः for the pleasure of the senses, च and, महा-उत्सवैः with great festivals, बभौ shone, भूः the earth, पक्व-सस्य-आढ्या rich with ripe crops, कलाभ्याम् by the two portions/expansions (Krishna and Balarama), नितराम् exceedingly, हरेः of Lord Hari

आग्रयण आग्रयण/ (अम्), n. oblation consisting of first-fruits at the end of the rainy season, src. Kosha Ashtadhyayi

Anvaya: पक्वसस्याढ्या भूः पुरग्रामेषु आग्रयणैः ऐन्द्रियैः महोत्सवैः च हरेः कलाभ्यां (कृष्णरामाभ्यां) नितरां बभौ।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: पक्वसस्याढ्या Rich with ripe crops, भूः the earth, पुरग्रामेषु in the cities and villages, आग्रयणैः with first-harvest sacrifices, ऐन्द्रियैः pleasing to the senses, महोत्सवैः with great festivals, च and, हरेः of Lord Hari, कलाभ्यां by the two expansions (Krishna and Balarama), नितरां exceedingly, बभौ shone.

Simple meaning: The earth was now heavy with ripe, golden crops. In every city and village, people celebrated with Agrayana sacrifices—offering the first grains to the gods—and held grand festivals that delighted the senses. Because of the presence of Lord Hari’s two expansions, Krishna and Balarama, the entire land shone with extraordinary beauty and prosperity.

The Climax of Abundance

This verse describes the physical and spiritual "harvest" of the autumn season:

  • पक्व-सस्य-आढ्या : The "wealth" of the earth is seen in its food. The transition from the green of the rains to the gold of the autumn harvest is complete.

  • आग्रयण : This refers to the traditional Vedic ritual where the first part of the new harvest is offered to the Devas before the community consumes it. It represents gratitude and the recognition that all resources belong to the Divine.

  • कलाभ्याम् : While the harvest made the land rich, the poet notes that the real reason for the "shining" (बभौ) of the earth was the presence of Krishna and Balarama. Their presence on earth turns a natural seasonal change into a divine celebration.

वणिङ्मुनिनृपस्नाता निर्गम्यार्थान् प्रपेदिरे ।

वर्षरुद्धा यथा सिद्धाः स्वपिण्डान् काल आगते ॥10.20.49॥

Word-by-word meaning: वणिक्-मुनि-नृप-स्नाताः the merchants, the sages, kings, and students (who have completed their vows), निर्गम्य coming out, अर्थान् their respective goals/objects, प्रपेदिरे obtained/pursued, वर्ष-रुद्धाः those who had been restricted by the rainy season, यथा just as, सिद्धाः perfected souls, स्व-पिण्डान् their respective bodies/destinations, काले आगते when the proper time arrives

Anvaya: वर्षरुद्धाः वणिङ्मुनिनृपस्नाताः निर्गम्य अर्थान् प्रपेदिरे, यथा काले आगते सिद्धाः स्वपिण्डान् (प्रपद्यन्ते)।

Literal meaning of the anvaya: वर्षरुद्धाः Restricted by the rains, वणिङ्मुनिनृपस्नाताः merchants, sages, kings, and students, निर्गम्य having come out, अर्थान् प्रपेदिरे pursued their goals, यथा just as, काले आगते when the time comes, सिद्धाः perfected souls, स्वपिण्डान् their own bodies/forms (obtain).

Simple meaning: The merchants, sages, kings, and students, who had all been restricted to their homes by the rains, now ventured out to pursue their respective goals. They were like perfected souls who, having waited for the right moment, leave their temporary shells to attain their destined spiritual forms.

Breaking the Stagnation

During the four months of the rainy season (Caturmasya), travel and major activities were traditionally halted. Autumn marks the "release" into action:

  • The Four Groups: 1. Merchants: Out for trade and wealth. 2. Sages: Traveling to teach and visit holy sites. 3. Kings: Venturing out for diplomacy or conquest (Vijaya-dashami). 4. Snatah (Students): Completing their studies or returning home.

  • The Philosophical Leap: The comparison to Siddhas is profound. A "perfected being" awaits the "proper time" (काले आगते) to transition. Just as the end of the rains allows the traveler to move toward his destination, the end of the "rainy season of material life" allows the soul to enter its true, eternal form (स्व-पिण्ड).

Thus ends the twentieth discourse named 'A description of the Rains and Autumn', in the first half of Book Ten of the great and glorious Bhagavata-Purana.
